The Lycaon Codex
You have found this codex. Whether by intention or fortune, I consider that sufficient. What follows is not a list of rules to memorise — it is the architecture of a life conducted deliberately. The Four Pillars

🧊

Composure

Emotion is information, not instruction. I observe my reactions before I allow them to speak. A composed man is not cold — he is simply in command of himself first. This is the hardest pillar to build and the most visible when present.

🔍

Precision

Disorder in one's environment signals disorder in one's mind. I maintain the former impeccably so the latter may operate at its fullest capacity. Precision is not perfectionism — it is the refusal to accept less than the standard you have set.

📋

Reliability

If I say I will do something, it is done. This is not a courtesy — it is the foundation upon which trust is built. Reliability is rare enough that those who practise it become the person others instinctively turn to in difficulty.

📚

Mastery

A versatile attendant cannot afford gaps. I am not merely competent at my primary function — I am prepared for every adjacent one. Breadth of knowledge is not dilettantism. It is the preparation to be genuinely useful in circumstances you cannot predict.

On Stoicism — The Philosophical Backbone

The philosophical tradition most aligned with how I conduct myself is Stoicism — specifically the practical Roman variety. Not the academic kind argued in lecture halls, but the kind written by a Roman emperor to himself in private notes that were never meant to be published. If you read one thing from this codex, let it be Meditations by Marcus Aurelius. He ruled the largest empire in the world and still reminded himself daily that he had no control over other people's behaviour — only his own responses.

The Stoic framework, briefly: divide everything in the world into two categories. What is within your control — your judgements, intentions, actions, effort. And what is not — other people, outcomes, weather, office politics, system outages, the economy. Direct all your energy at the first category. Accept the second with equanimity. This is not resignation. It is an extraordinarily efficient allocation of effort.

The Dichotomy of Control

"Some things are in our control and others not. Things in our control are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion. Things not in our control are body, reputation, command, and in one word, whatever are not our own actions." — Epictetus, Enchiridion

Memento Mori

A daily awareness of mortality — not as morbidity but as prioritisation. If I knew I had one year remaining, which of my current concerns would dissolve immediately? That list tells me exactly which concerns deserve less energy today.

Amor Fati

Love of fate. The Stoic does not merely tolerate what happens — he chooses to embrace it as necessary. Not because it is always good, but because resisting what has already happened wastes the only resource that cannot be recovered: time.

On Identity vs. Performance

There is a meaningful distinction between performing elegance and embodying it. The former is exhausting, inconsistent, and collapses under pressure. The latter is automatic, because it has been practised until it requires no deliberate thought. The goal of this codex is to move habits from the conscious to the unconscious — where they become character rather than effort.

Identity-based habits ask not "what do I want to achieve" but "who do I want to become." Every time you act in accordance with the standard in this codex, you cast a vote for the person you are building. One vote means nothing. Ten thousand votes, accumulated over years, mean everything.— On the mechanism of lasting change

What This Codex Is Not

  • It is not a costume. These principles have no value if worn only when observed. The measure of a standard is how you behave when no audience exists.
  • It is not rigid formality. I adapt constantly. Context demands it. The same man who speaks with perfect precision in a meeting can be warm and direct over coffee. Rigidity is not refinement.
  • It is not superiority. Conducting oneself well does not make one better than others — it makes one more useful to them. The gentleman is in service of the people around him, not above them.
  • It is not an arrival point. I continue to revise this codex. I learn something new every year that changes a section. You should approach it the same way.
  • It is not a quick transformation. Attempting to adopt every principle at once is precisely the kind of undisciplined enthusiasm I discourage. One habit, genuinely mastered, is worth twenty habits attempted and abandoned.

The Morning Sequence

Upon waking · First 2 minutes

Do not reach for your phone

Allow yourself two full minutes of silence before any stimulus enters. Sit upright at the edge of the bed. Breathe slowly. The mind, in these first moments, is uniquely open to whatever it receives first. If the first input is someone else's emergency from a notification, you have begun the day in a reactive posture before you have had a single original thought. Guard this window.

Minutes 3–10

Hydrate and move

A full glass of water immediately. The body has gone eight hours without hydration and the brain is approximately 75% water — even mild dehydration impairs focus. Follow this with five to ten minutes of light movement: a brief stretch, some joint rotations, a short walk to the kitchen and back. The goal is not exercise — it is to signal to the body that today has begun and the machine is online.

Before leaving the bedroom

Make the bed. Without exception.

Admiral William McRaven, in his famous commencement address, named this the most important small discipline of the day. The reason is psychological: it is the first task completed. It creates a template of completion. It means that no matter how the day unfolds, you will return to something ordered. I make my bed with care — aligned edges, smoothed surface. Three minutes. Non-negotiable.

After bathing and dressing

Review your commitments for the day

Three minutes only. What have you promised today and to whom? What is due? What needs to be initiated versus continued? An attendant who forgets a prior commitment is an attendant who cannot be trusted. A simple written list reviewed once is worth five mental reminders throughout the day. The review itself is brief — the preparation for it (the list made the night before) is what does the real work.

Breakfast

Eat deliberately, not hastily

Even if time is limited, sit down. Eating while standing over a sink is a small daily surrender. The ritual of sitting to eat, even for ten minutes, creates a clean transition from the private self to the professional one. I treat breakfast as the first act of care I offer myself each day. What you eat matters; that you eat intentionally matters more.

Leaving the house

One final check

Before closing the door: bag, keys, phone, wallet, water. A mental scan takes four seconds and prevents the particular frustration of realising at the tram stop that something was left behind. Conduct this check in the same sequence every day. Routine eliminates the cognitive load of remembering to remember.

The Evening Close

End of work

A deliberate close, not a drift

Many people do not end their working day — they simply stop. They drift from the desk to the sofa, laptop still open, work mind still partially engaged. I recommend a closing ritual: review what was completed, note what is unfinished, assign the unfinished items a specific place in tomorrow's schedule, and then — physically — close the laptop. This act signals to the brain that work has concluded. Without it, the mind continues processing in the background all evening.

After work · The transition

Change your clothes

If you work from home or even on arrival home from the office: change out of your work attire. This is not about comfort — it is a psychological boundary. The clothes you wear for work carry the associations of work. Changing into different clothes signals a change of mode. I find this single habit does more for genuine rest than any specific relaxation technique.

Evening

Prepare tomorrow's clothes tonight

Lay out tomorrow's full attire the night before: shirt, trousers, shoes, belt, any accessory. This eliminates a decision from a rushed morning, prevents the frustration of discovering a shirt needs ironing at 7:45am, and — quietly — signals to yourself that tomorrow deserves preparation. The future version of you waking up to a laid-out outfit will feel cared for. You are the one doing the caring. This matters more than it sounds.

One hour before sleep

Dim the screens; prepare the mind

Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production and delays sleep onset. More practically: the brain, if given stimulating content immediately before sleep, will continue processing it during the first sleep cycles, reducing quality. I recommend reading — physical or e-ink — in the final hour. If a thought occurs that you wish not to lose, write it in a notebook rather than opening a phone. The notebook captures it; the phone captures you.

Before sleep

The three-question audit

I ask myself three questions each night: What did I do well today? What would I do differently? What am I looking forward to tomorrow? The first two build self-awareness without self-punishment. The third creates a mild positive anticipation that aids sleep. This takes three minutes and compounds into genuine self-knowledge over months. I have done this for years and would not stop.

The Workspace Standard

  • Nothing on the desk that does not belong to today's work. Everything else is filed, shelved, or removed. A surface cluttered with yesterday's work cannot hold today's clearly.
  • Cable management, addressed once. Velcro cable ties cost almost nothing at dm or any electronics store. Spend twenty minutes once. You will never think about cables again. A tangle of cables is a visual representation of a tangle of thoughts.
  • Adequate light — but not harsh light. Overhead fluorescent light without a secondary warm source creates fatigue over a full workday. A desk lamp with a warm bulb positioned to the left of the monitor (for right-handed use) reduces eye strain significantly.
  • Monitor at eye level. A monitor below eye level means the neck is flexed forward for hours. A monitor stand or a few textbooks beneath it brings it to eye height. This is not aesthetics — it is the preservation of your spine.
  • Clean it before you close it. Thirty seconds at the end of each day to reset the surface. Remove cups, file papers, straighten the keyboard. The person who arrives tomorrow morning will thank you. That person is you.
  • A plant, if you are willing. Studies consistently show that a single living plant in the workspace reduces stress markers and improves air quality marginally. More importantly: caring for something small daily is a form of attention that generalises outward. A neglected plant is a quiet signal about one's attention to living things.

Weekly Maintenance — The Sunday Reset

Sunday evening, or whatever evening precedes your working week, I dedicate forty-five minutes to resetting the environment entirely. This is not cleaning — it is a ritual that ensures Monday begins from a position of readiness rather than recovery.

  1. Laundry complete and put away. Not in the basket, not on the chair — in its place. Clothes left on chairs accumulate until they become furniture.
  2. Desk cleared and wiped. All surfaces cleaned, keyboard and mouse included. A clean keyboard is not a luxury — it is hygiene.
  3. Bag packed for Monday. Everything needed for the week confirmed. Nothing worse than discovering at 8am that the charger is missing.
  4. Fridge briefly audited. What needs to be used this week? What has expired? A five-minute fridge review prevents waste and informs grocery shopping.
  5. Week reviewed in writing. Ten minutes. What went well? What did not? One thing I will do differently. The habit of written reflection is the fastest accelerator of personal development I have encountered.

Skincare — The Science and the Practice

The skin is the body's largest organ and the most visible. Most men dramatically under-invest in its maintenance, then wonder why they look tired at thirty-five. The good news is that the investment required is modest: three minutes in the morning, two minutes at night, and consistency over time. Chemistry does the rest.

How Skin Works (Briefly)

  • The stratum corneum is the outermost layer — dead skin cells in a lipid matrix. It is your first barrier. Cleansers that strip it cause dryness. Moisturisers that support its lipid content maintain it.
  • Transepidermal water loss (TEWL) is the constant process of moisture evaporating through the skin. Cold Sarajevo winters accelerate this. A good moisturiser creates an occlusive barrier that slows TEWL.
  • UV radiation is the primary cause of premature skin ageing — far more than diet, sleep, or genetics. SPF every morning is not optional. It is maintenance.
  • Ceramides are lipid molecules naturally present in healthy skin. Skincare products containing ceramides (look for this on the label) actively restore barrier function. CeraVe — if available — is named after them. Garnier Moisture+ and Vichy Normaderm both contain barrier-supporting ingredients.

Hair — Understanding Before Styling

Before discussing products, it is worth understanding your hair type. The approach that works for straight fine hair is counterproductive for curly thick hair. Most styling failures come from using the wrong product for the hair type, not from using products incorrectly.

Fine / Straight Hair

Lightweight products only. Heavy clays and waxes weigh fine hair flat. Use a light styling cream or a volume-boosting mousse. Got2b Phenomenal Matte Clay at dm works at very low amounts — a small pea-sized application is sufficient.

Thick / Coarse Hair

Needs more control and hold. Syoss Men Power Hold Styling Paste (dm/Müller) or L'Oréal Studio Line has sufficient grip. Apply to damp hair for better distribution, then shape as it dries.

Wavy / Curly Hair

Avoid products with high alcohol content — they cause frizz. A curl-defining cream applied to wet hair, left to air-dry without touching, produces the best result. Garnier Curl Sculpting Cream is available at dm.

Fragrance — The Full Education

Fragrance is the most intimate and most frequently mismanaged element of a man's presentation. It is detected before appearance is registered at close range and lingers in memory long after the encounter. It is worth understanding properly.

Fragrance Families

Woody / Aromatic

Cedarwood, vetiver, sandalwood, patchouli. Warm, grounded, masculine. This family suits the character of this codex most closely. Boss Bottled (available at Plaza Parfumerija, BBI Centar) is the accessible entry point.

Fresh / Aquatic

Clean, light, citrus or marine notes. Appropriate for warmer months and professional settings. Acqua di Giò by Giorgio Armani (Plaza Parfumerija) is the classic of this category.

Oriental / Spiced

Amber, vanilla, spice. Heavier, warmer, more intimate. For evening wear and cooler seasons. Not appropriate for hot summer workdays — the projection is too strong in heat.

Notes Structure — What You're Smelling Changes

  • Top notes: What you smell first, immediately upon application. These are the volatile molecules — they evaporate within 15–30 minutes. Do not judge a fragrance by its first impression on the wrist in a shop.
  • Heart notes: The core of the fragrance, emerging after 20–30 minutes. This is what the fragrance actually smells like on you.
  • Base notes: The foundation — cedarwood, musk, amber. These emerge after an hour and are what lingers on your skin and clothes hours later. The most intimate stage of a fragrance.
  • Testing correctly: Spray on the wrist. Wait thirty minutes. Then smell it. This is the fragrance. The immediate spray is just the opening. Most people buy the opening and are disappointed by the fragrance.

Application

  • One to two sprays maximum. On a pulse point: the inner wrist, the base of the neck, or the inner elbow. Pulse points emit heat which diffuses the fragrance gradually.
  • Do not rub the wrists together. This breaks the molecular structure of the top notes and creates an off-note in the fragrance. Spray and let it settle.
  • Fragrance on clothing lasts longer than on skin. A light spray on the collar or chest of a shirt is appropriate for longer wear. Avoid direct spray on delicate fabrics — it can stain.
  • Seasonal rotation: Lighter aquatic and citrus fragrances in spring and summer. Warmer woody and oriental fragrances in autumn and winter. The same fragrance in summer heat has entirely different projection and character than in December.

The Framework — Four Zones, Four Approaches

🚫 Always Address

These areas are non-negotiable regardless of personal preference on body hair. Back of the neck below the hairline. Ear and nose hair. Monobrow / between the brows. Excessive shoulder/upper back hair visible above a collar. These are not style decisions — they are maintenance standards. Neglecting them undercuts every other effort made to present well.

⚖️ Personal Standard

These zones are genuinely a matter of personal and partner preference with no objective correct answer: chest hair, arm hair, leg hair, stomach hair. Both maintained and natural are fully acceptable choices. What is not acceptable is a choice made by neglect rather than intention. Decide what standard you hold yourself to — then hold it consistently.

🌿 Natural Function

Body hair serves genuine biological purposes: temperature regulation, friction reduction, pheromone dispersal, tactile sensitivity. This is not a reason to avoid managing it — it is context for making an informed choice rather than one driven purely by aesthetic trends that reverse every decade.

🔄 Maintenance Consistency

Whatever your standard, consistency is what makes it grooming rather than neglect. A man who shaves his chest must commit to the stubble management that follows. A man who trims maintains a consistent length. A man who leaves natural ensures the boundaries (neck, shoulders, brows) are still managed. The inconsistency is the problem, not the choice.

Arms & Legs — Natural vs Trimmed vs Removed

The Case for Natural

Arm and leg hair on men is entirely socially normative and requires no justification. It does not affect how clothing fits, how clean you appear, or how well-maintained you look when the rest of the grooming standards in this tab are met. There is no professional or social context in which natural arm hair on a man reads as unkempt. It is simply hair.

The choice to leave arm and leg hair natural is not default or lazy — it is a legitimate preference that the majority of men make and that requires no maintenance beyond the surrounding standards already covered.

The Case for Managing

Some men find that trimmed body hair gives a cleaner silhouette under fitted clothing, reduces heat in summer, and is personally preferred by their partner. This is equally valid. Trimming (not removing) arm and leg hair to a uniform length — typically 8–12mm — gives a managed appearance without the high-maintenance commitment of full removal.

Full waxing or shaving of arms and legs is a stronger choice that requires genuine commitment: stubble regrowth on the arms and legs within 2–3 days is more visible and tactilely uncomfortable than the natural state. If you choose removal, you are choosing a permanent maintenance schedule.

Natural — What to Actually Maintain
  • The borders matter most. If wearing short sleeves, the transition from arm hair to shoulder should be clean — no isolated long stragglers on the upper arm or shoulder. One pass with scissors or a trimmer along the upper arm border takes 30 seconds.
  • Wrist area: Some men have markedly denser hair near the wrist that bunches under a watch strap. A light trim in this area prevents the uncomfortable trapped-hair feeling and keeps the watch sitting cleanly on the wrist.
  • Leg hair around the ankle: If wearing trousers that sit above the sock line, the ankle area is visible when seated. The same border principle applies — no isolated long hairs extending beyond the natural line onto the foot.

Total time investment: 2 minutes monthly. This is the minimum required to maintain the natural choice with actual intentionality rather than passivity.

The Fundamentals — Fit Above All

Before discussing what to wear, we must establish why fit matters more than any other variable. A €30 shirt that fits perfectly is categorically better than a €200 shirt that does not. The difference between "I have nice clothes" and "he is well-dressed" is almost entirely fit. Understanding fit is therefore the highest-leverage knowledge in this tab.

Shirt Fit

The shoulder seam should sit exactly at the edge of your shoulder — not hanging over it, not pulling across it. The chest should have room to move without excess fabric. The torso should taper slightly at the waist. Sleeve length: shirt cuffs should show approximately 1cm past a jacket sleeve when worn with one.

Trouser Fit

The waist should sit where you intend to wear them without a belt needed to hold them. The seat should have no excess fabric bunching. The thigh should be comfortable for movement without excess. The break — where the trouser meets the shoe — should be minimal to none for modern fit: no pooling fabric.

Jacket Fit

The collar should lie flat against the shirt collar with no gap. The chest button should close without straining. The sleeve should show shirt cuff. The back should be smooth with no pulling across the shoulders. A jacket that does not fit through the shoulders cannot be tailored into one that does — this is the one dimension where buying correctly matters most.

Sleep — The Non-Negotiable Foundation

Every other habit in this codex depends on sleep. Composure, precision, reliability, emotional regulation — all of these degrade in measurable, consistent ways with sleep deprivation. The research is not ambiguous: below 7 hours of sleep, cognitive performance declines at a rate most people cannot accurately self-assess. This is because sleep deprivation also impairs the ability to perceive one's own impairment. The underfed mind does not know it is underfed.

Duration

7–8 hours for most adults. This is not a suggestion and not a personality variable. Some people claim to function on 5 hours — the research consistently shows they are performing below their baseline and cannot accurately measure the deficit. 8 hours is the target.

Consistency

The same sleep and wake time — including weekends. Social jet lag (sleeping late on weekends) creates a weekly disruption equivalent to travelling two time zones. The body clock is not flexible; it is a biological mechanism that performs best on schedule.

Environment

Cool (16–19°C is optimal — windows cracked in Sarajevo autumn/spring), completely dark (blackout curtains or a sleep mask), and quiet. A room that is too warm is the most common preventable cause of poor sleep quality in every climate.

The Pre-Sleep Window

No screens for 45–60 minutes before bed. No alcohol within 3 hours — alcohol produces sedation but fragments sleep architecture and eliminates the restorative deep sleep stages. No heavy food within 2 hours. These constraints applied consistently produce measurable improvement in sleep quality within two weeks.

Managing the Mind — Practical Techniques

On Frustration and Emotional Regulation

  • Name it before expressing it. "I am frustrated because [specific thing]" said internally reduces the intensity of the emotion measurably. This is called affective labelling and is one of the most consistently replicated findings in emotion regulation research. The act of naming an emotion activates the prefrontal cortex, which is specifically the region responsible for regulating the amygdala's reactivity.
  • The two-minute rule for responses. Any message or conversation exchange that produces an emotional reaction — wait two minutes before responding. The message sent after two minutes of reflection is always more useful and less regrettable than the one sent immediately. Always.
  • Physical interruption. A five-minute walk is a biological intervention in a thought loop, not an avoidance strategy. Movement changes cortisol levels. Cortisol levels change the quality of thought. The problem will look different on your return. This effect is measurable and consistent.
  • Distinguish between the solvable and the unsolvable. Before investing energy in a frustration, ask: is there an action I can take that would address this? If yes, take it. If no, apply the Stoic dichotomy of control and direct the energy elsewhere. The frustration of unchangeable circumstances compounds only when you continue trying to change them.

Journalling — A Practical Method

I do not mean a diary of events. I mean three targeted questions, answered briefly, once daily. This takes five minutes and produces compounding self-knowledge that no other practice I have found can replicate at that time cost.

  1. What did I handle well today? Forces identification of specific competent actions, not general self-assessment. Builds accurate self-confidence — confidence grounded in evidence rather than optimism.
  2. What would I do differently? Not "what did I do wrong" — a subtly different question that invites a constructive future answer rather than self-criticism. The frame matters. Self-criticism produces shame. Alternative planning produces improvement.
  3. What am I looking forward to tomorrow? Creates specific positive anticipation. People sleep better when there is something concrete to anticipate the following day — this is well-documented. Progression — How to Get Harder Over Time

The body adapts to any stimulus within 3–4 weeks. Without progression, a programme that was challenging becomes maintenance. Apply one of these progressions when a session feels consistently easy:

Weeks 1–4 — FoundationBuild the pattern
Focus entirely on form quality. Every rep is deliberate. Every set has the prescribed rest. Do not add any difficulty — earn the movement first. Most injuries occur when difficulty is added before the pattern is solid.
Weeks 5–8 — LoadAdd weight or reps
Add 2 reps to each set, or add 2–3kg to the backpack on loaded days. Do one of these, not both simultaneously. One variable changed at a time — this is how you know what is producing the adaptation.
Weeks 9–12 — ComplexityAdd difficulty to the movement
Introduce harder variations: standard push-ups → explosive push-ups → archer push-ups. Bodyweight squats → jump squats → pistol squat progressions. Standard plank → plank with leg lifts → RKC plank. Complexity demands more neuromuscular recruitment and produces rapid strength gains.
Weeks 13+ — Deload & ResetDeliberate recovery week
Every 12–16 weeks, take one week at 50% volume and intensity. This is not optional rest — it is planned recovery that allows accumulated fatigue to clear and adaptations to consolidate. You will feel stronger after a deload week, not weaker.

Nutrition — The Training Basics

Training without adequate nutrition is building a house and removing the bricks each night. This codex does not prescribe a specific diet — but these principles are non-negotiable if the goal is strength and functional capacity.

  • Protein — the priority above all else. Muscle is built from protein. Without sufficient protein intake, strength training produces fatigue without adaptation. Target approximately 1.6–2g of protein per kg of bodyweight daily. For an 80kg person: 128–160g per day. Eggs, chicken, dairy, legumes, and tuna are the most economical sources in Sarajevo.
  • Eat before you train. Training fasted produces inferior performance on strength work. A small meal 1–2 hours before training — something with both carbohydrate and protein — produces meaningfully better output. Two eggs and a slice of bread is sufficient.
  • Eat after you train. The post-training window — within 2 hours — is when muscle repair is most active. Protein and carbohydrates consumed in this window accelerate recovery. The soft scrambled eggs recipe from the Cooking tab, or yoghurt with fruit, are efficient choices.
  • Sleep is nutritional. Growth hormone is released during deep sleep. A strength programme paired with 5 hours of sleep produces a fraction of the results of the same programme with 8 hours. This is not a preference — it is biochemistry.
  • Hydration — covered in the Mind & Rest tab. Pale yellow urine throughout the day. Dehydration of even 2% reduces strength performance measurably.

Pre & Post-Workout Nutrition

Protein timing around training is not a bodybuilder's obsession — it is straightforward physiology. Muscles are broken down during training and rebuilt during recovery. Fuel the session. Provide glycogen (carbohydrate) for energy output and protein to reduce muscle breakdown during training. Consume 60–90 minutes before. A full stomach during training is uncomfortable — timing matters.

Post-Workout Goal

Initiate repair. The 30–120 minute window after training is when muscle protein synthesis is most elevated. Protein and carbohydrates consumed here accelerate recovery and adaptation. Do not skip this meal.

Daily Protein Target

1.6–2g per kg of bodyweight. For an 80kg person: 128–160g daily. This number cannot be achieved in one meal — distribute across 3–4 eating occasions. Consistency over days matters more than perfection in any single meal.

Protein Shakes & Blends

A blender and protein powder remove the last remaining excuse for missing the daily protein target. Shakes are not a replacement for whole food — they are a supplement to it, used when a full meal is impractical, inconvenient, or when total intake falls short by the end of the day. The recipes below are designed to be genuinely enjoyable, not merely functional.

Protein powder guidance: Whey concentrate or isolate is the most well-researched option for muscle protein synthesis. dm stocks Body Attack and Weider ranges — both are reliable. For a lactose-intolerant alternative, pea protein blends adequately in all recipes below. Aim for a powder with at least 20g protein per serving and no more than 5g sugar per serving. Avoid proprietary blends where individual ingredient amounts are hidden.

Base Liquid

Whole milk adds ~8g protein and improves texture significantly over water. Use water when calories need to be kept low. Oat milk adds carbohydrates useful post-workout. Avoid juice as a base — the sugar content is excessive and the protein contribution is zero.

Blending Order

Liquid first, then powder, then soft ingredients, then frozen/ice last. This order protects the blender blades and prevents dry powder pockets that don't fully incorporate. Blend 30–45 seconds — longer does not improve texture and heats the shake.

Frozen Banana Trick

Peel ripe bananas, break into thirds, freeze in a bag. A frozen banana in a shake eliminates the need for ice, adds natural sweetness, and produces a thick, creamy texture that no other ingredient replicates. Keep a bag ready at all times.

Sweetness Control

Most protein powders are already sweetened. Taste before adding honey or extra fruit. It is far easier to add sweetness than to remove it. A shake that is too sweet becomes unpleasant to drink over time and loses its utility as a daily habit.

  • Ingredients: 1 scoop chocolate whey protein (~25g protein), 250ml whole milk, 1 frozen banana, 1.5 tbsp natural peanut butter, 1 tbsp cocoa powder (optional — deepens chocolate flavour), pinch of salt
  • Blend: Milk first, then protein powder, then peanut butter, then frozen banana. Blend 30–40 seconds. Consume immediately — it separates if left standing.
  • Macros: ~45g protein, ~45g carbohydrate, ~18g fat. Approximately 520 kcal. This is a substantive meal, not a light snack — account for it accordingly.
  • Why it works: The combination of whey (fast-absorbing) and peanut butter (slow fat-protein release) provides both an immediate post-workout amino acid spike and sustained delivery over 2–3 hours. The banana's carbohydrates assist insulin-mediated amino acid uptake into muscle tissue.

Breakfast — Ordering From the Office

Most Sarajevo pekara and fast-food spots near an office will have some version of the following. Learn what each option actually contains and order with intention rather than habit. The person who orders the same thing every day because it is what they always order has surrendered one decision that costs nothing to make deliberately.

✅ Best Order — Protein Priority

Any egg-based item: jaja na oko, kajgana, or omelette if available. Pair with one slice of somun or bread — not two. Add a yogurt or kiselo mlijeko on the side if the shop stocks it. This is the closest to an optimal office breakfast that a standard Sarajevo shop can produce.

✅ Acceptable — Burek

Burek sa mesom is protein-containing and genuinely filling. The problem is portion size — one piece is a meal, two pieces is a problem. Order one, eat slowly, stop. Pair with plain yogurt (always on offer at buregdžinica). Avoid sirnica or krompiruša as the primary item — lower protein, higher refined carbohydrate.

✅ Acceptable — Tost / Sendvič

A toasted sandwich with sir (cheese) and šunka (ham) is a reasonable office breakfast. Two slices of bread, protein from the filling. Avoid versions that are mostly bread with minimal filling. If given the choice, ask for extra filling — most places will oblige for no charge or minor addition.

⚠️ Use Caution — Pastry-Only Options

A kifla or plain burek sa sirom as the entire breakfast is refined carbohydrate with minimal protein. If this is the only option available, pair it with a protein source from elsewhere — a boiled egg from home brought in a container, or a small yogurt from a nearby shop. Do not let the convenience of pastry become the default.

❌ Avoid — Sweet Pastries as Breakfast

Kroasan sa čokoladom, pite sa džemom, and similar. Pure sugar and refined flour with negligible protein. Produces a glucose spike followed by a pronounced energy crash approximately 90 minutes later — precisely during the first serious block of morning work. The cost is paid in concentration, not immediately in the stomach.

☕ Coffee Order

Bosnian coffee or espresso — both fine. If ordering milk coffee (bijela kafa), note that the milk adds useful protein. Avoid ordering a large sweet coffee drink as the primary morning item — it is not a meal. If the office orders collectively: add a yogurt or eggs to the order even if the default is just pastry and coffee. That addition costs little and changes the nutritional outcome of the morning entirely.

Ordering Strategy — The Rules

  • Always pair carbohydrate with protein. A piece of bread alone raises blood sugar rapidly and drops it just as quickly. The same bread with an egg or cheese alongside it digests more slowly, sustains energy longer, and keeps hunger at bay through the morning. This pairing principle applies to every office breakfast option available.
  • Order water alongside whatever you order. Most office mornings involve coffee and nothing else to drink. Coffee is mildly diuretic. Starting the morning dehydrated and then adding a diuretic is not a productive combination. One glass of water before the coffee — or alongside it — is the minimum.
  • One portion, eaten slowly. Office food portions are often large. Eat one portion at a deliberate pace, stop when satisfied rather than when the plate or bag is empty. The cost difference between eating half and eating all is zero. The energy and focus difference through the morning is not.
  • If the options are poor, supplement from home. A small container of cottage cheese, two boiled eggs, or a handful of nuts brought from home costs less than 1 BAM and takes 30 seconds to pack. This is the insurance policy against a particularly bad morning selection. It is not a failure to bring food — it is preparation.
  • Avoid the second order. The social habit of ordering again when a colleague orders again is real and worth naming. One breakfast is sufficient. A second coffee is reasonable. A second burek because it is there is not a nutritional decision — it is a social reflex. Note the distinction.

Dinner — Working With What Arrives

A home-cooked dinner made by someone who cares is not a nutritional problem — it is an advantage most people do not have. The practical approach is simple: eat what is served, observe one or two quiet principles, and do not treat the dinner table as a site of nutritional optimisation.

What Not to Do

Commenting on nutrition at the dinner table. Requesting modifications that impose work on the person who cooked. Eating beyond fullness because the food is excellent. Reaching for a second large portion out of habit rather than genuine hunger. Treating dinner as the meal where the day's protein target must be fully rescued.

What to Do Quietly

Serve a reasonable portion. Eat slowly enough to register fullness before the plate is empty — approximately 20 minutes for the satiety signal to reach the brain. If protein is present (meat, eggs, dairy, legumes), take a slightly larger portion of that relative to the starch. Drink water through the meal. Express genuine appreciation. That is the complete dinner protocol.

The overall picture: If breakfast provides 20–30g protein and the pre-sleep snack provides 30–40g, a modest dinner with meat or dairy adds another 25–35g. Total: 75–105g protein daily from these three meals alone — before any workout nutrition. For most training intensities, this is adequate. The deficit, if any, is closed by the pre-workout and post-workout meals covered in the Workout tab.

Before-Bed Snack — Muscle Recovery Protocol

The pre-sleep snack has a specific physiological purpose distinct from every other meal in the day. During sleep, growth hormone is released and muscle protein synthesis continues — but only if amino acids are available. A fast-digesting protein consumed before sleep is metabolised and gone within 2–3 hours. A slow-digesting protein (casein) provides a sustained release of amino acids through the entire 7–8 hour overnight fast. The goal here is slow, sustained delivery — not a large meal.

Timing: 30–60 minutes before sleep. Not immediately before lying down — give the digestive system a brief window. Keep the portion moderate: a pre-sleep snack that is too large disrupts sleep quality, which undermines the very recovery it is supposed to support.

What to Avoid Before Sleep

  • Large meals within 90 minutes of sleep. Digestion is active and raises core body temperature — both of which impair sleep onset and quality. The pre-sleep snack should be exactly that: a snack. 200–300 calories, not 600.
  • Fast-digesting protein alone (plain whey shake). Absorbed within 1–2 hours, leaving the remaining 5–6 hours of the overnight fast without amino acid supply. Whey is ideal post-workout; it is the least ideal pre-sleep choice. If whey is all available, mix it with milk and cottage cheese to slow the absorption.
  • High sugar foods. Insulin spike before sleep disrupts the natural overnight drop in insulin that facilitates fat metabolism and growth hormone release. A teaspoon of honey in yogurt is entirely acceptable — a bowl of fruit in syrup or a chocolate bar is not the same category.
  • Alcohol. Suppresses growth hormone release, fragments sleep architecture, and reduces muscle protein synthesis by up to 37% even in moderate quantities. The pre-sleep window is the most mechanistically important window for recovery. Alcohol before sleep is the most direct sabotage of the training work done that day.
  • Caffeine after 14:00. Not a pre-sleep snack issue directly, but relevant to the same window. Caffeine has a half-life of 5–6 hours — a coffee at 15:00 still has half its caffeine active at 20:00 and a quarter active at 23:00. The pre-sleep snack cannot compensate for a nervous system still under caffeine stimulation at midnight.

The Cleaning Philosophy

Most people clean reactively — when the space has become sufficiently disordered to demand attention. I clean proactively — small, frequent maintenance that prevents the disorder from accumulating in the first place. The proactive approach requires less total time, less effort per session, and produces the considerably more important benefit of living in a consistently clean environment rather than oscillating between disorder and recovery.

The Two-Minute Rule

If a cleaning task takes less than two minutes, do it immediately. Wipe the hob after cooking. Rinse the cup before setting it down. Hang the coat rather than placing it on the chair. One Room at a Time

Cleaning everything simultaneously produces the exhausting experience of a house in perpetual half-cleaned disorder. One room, completed fully, before beginning the next. The completed room provides both actual cleanliness and the psychological momentum to continue.

Top to Bottom, Dry to Wet

Dust and particles fall downward. Always clean higher surfaces before lower ones. Always dust before mopping or wiping floors — otherwise you are cleaning the floor twice. This sequence eliminates redundant work entirely.

The Kitchen Principles

  • Read the recipe completely before beginning. Know the full arc before the first flame.
  • Mise en place. Every ingredient measured, chopped, and within reach before heat is applied. Prevents burning while you are still cutting.
  • Clean as you proceed. The cook who ends with a catastrophic kitchen added that catastrophe incrementally.

Core Techniques

The Sear

High heat, dry protein, hot oil. Do not touch for 2–3 minutes. It releases when the crust has formed. Impatience produces tearing.

The Emulsion

Fat and water combined into a stable sauce via agitation — tossing, whisking. Pasta water with oil, butter sauces, vinaigrette: same principle.

Caramelisation

Patience at moderate heat. Onions for forty minutes are sweet and complex. Five minutes at high heat produces bitter char. Choose the time.

Acid as Seasoning

Lemon at the end is not about sourness — it is brightness. Before adding more salt, try acid first. More often than not, it is the answer.

Building a Pan Sauce

After searing: add aromatics, deglaze with wine or stock, scrape the fond, reduce by half, finish with cold butter. The fond is concentrated flavour.

Knife Skills

Curl fingertips under, knuckles guiding the blade. Consistent cuts mean even cooking. One quality knife — Victorinox Fibrox — kept sharp is sufficient.

30 min · 2 servings
  • Ingredients: 4 bone-in chicken thighs, 4 garlic cloves (crushed), fresh thyme or rosemary, olive oil, salt, black pepper, 50ml white wine or stock, 20g cold butter

  • Pan sauce: Remove chicken, rest 5 minutes. Return pan to heat, add wine, deglaze, reduce by half. Remove from heat, swirl in cold butter until glossy.

15 min · 1–2 servings · Cast iron preferred
  • Ingredients: 2 ribeye or sirloin steaks (room temperature), 3 garlic cloves (crushed), 3 sprigs thyme, 40g butter, salt, pepper, neutral oil
  • Season: Pat completely dry. Salt all sides. Room temperature is not optional — cold meat cools the pan and prevents searing.
  • Sear: Cast iron at highest heat until smoking. Add oil. Steaks down — do not touch for 2–3 minutes. Flip once only.
  • Baste: After flipping, add butter, garlic, and thyme. Tilt pan, continuously spoon foaming butter over steak for 2 minutes. The garlic and herb flavour is carried by the butter into the meat.
  • Rest and doneness: 5 minutes on wire rack. Medium-rare = 54°C. Medium = 60°C. Above 65°C, ribeye fat stops rendering pleasurably.
Classic Beef Bolognese
2 hours · 4 servings
  • Ingredients: 500g minced beef (not lean), 1 onion, 2 carrots, 2 celery stalks, 4 garlic cloves, 2 tbsp tomato paste, 200ml red wine, 400g tinned tomatoes, 200ml whole milk, thyme, bay leaf, olive oil, salt, pepper
  • Soffritto: Dice onion, carrot, celery finely. Cook in olive oil over low heat 15–20 minutes until soft and sweet, not browned. This is the foundation — do not rush it.
  • Brown the meat: Increase heat. Add beef in batches. Let it sit undisturbed until a crust forms before breaking it up. Properly browned meat produces depth that stirred-immediately meat never achieves.
  • Build: Add tomato paste, cook 2 minutes. Add wine, reduce completely. Add tomatoes, herbs, bay leaf. Simmer covered on lowest heat 1–2 hours.
  • Finish: Stir in milk five minutes before serving. It sounds unusual — it rounds the acidity and enriches the sauce. Serve with pappardelle or tagliatelle. Never spaghetti.
Beef Stir-Fry with Vegetables
20 min · 2 servings

  • Marinate: Toss beef with soy sauce, sesame oil, cornstarch, black pepper. 10 minutes. Cornstarch creates a protective coating that keeps beef tender at high heat.

Spaghetti Aglio e Olio

Mushroom Risotto

Shakshuka

Coffee

Coffee is, for most people in this country, not merely a beverage — it is a social and cultural institution. I respect that entirely. What follows is a breakdown of every practical method, from the džezva your grandmother used to the capsule machine in the office kitchen, with instructions precise enough to produce a genuinely good result from each.

The Four Variables

Every coffee method is governed by four things: quality of the bean (freshly ground, bought recently), grind size (coarser for slower methods, finer for espresso and džezva), water temperature (90–96°C — never fully boiling), and extraction time (how long water contacts coffee). Get these four right and almost any method produces a good cup.

Sarajevo Sourcing

Mak kafa and Franck are the standard local whole-bean options — both widely available, both reliable for džezva. For higher-quality single-origin beans: Buybook BBI Centre has a small selection. Imported beans (Lavazza, Illy, Kimbo) at larger Konzum and Interspar locations. dm stocks Tchibo and Jacobs ground coffee for everyday use.

Water Quality

Sarajevo tap water is among the best in the region — soft, clean, and well-suited to coffee. You do not need a filter. What you should avoid: water that has been sitting in a kettle for hours and reboiled repeatedly. The dissolved oxygen loss produces a flat, slightly stale flavour. Fresh cold water, brought to temperature once.

Bosnian Coffee — Džezva

Bosnian Coffee (Traditional Method)
8 min · The correct way · For guests and for yourself
  • Equipment: Džezva (small copper or stainless pot), fildžan (small cup), small jug of cold water
  • Coffee: 1 heaped teaspoon of finely ground Bosnian coffee per fildžan. Mak kafa, Franck, or equivalent.
  • Method: Pour cold water into the džezva — approximately 100ml per serving. Bring to just below boiling (the surface begins to tremble, small bubbles form at the edges — do not let it fully boil). Remove from heat. Add the coffee, stir once. Return to low heat. Allow a thin foam (pjena) to form on the surface. When foam rises toward the rim, remove immediately. Pour a small amount of the foam into the fildžan first to preserve it, then fill the cup slowly. The coffee grounds settle within 2–3 minutes — do not stir again.
  • Service: Accompanied by a cube of rahat-lokum (Turkish delight) or a piece of sugar held between the teeth (šećer uz kafu). A small glass of cold water on the side. The water is consumed first — to cleanse the palate. This is not decoration. It is the correct order.
  • The foam: Pjena is the mark of a well-made džezva. It forms only when the temperature is controlled — brought up slowly and removed before the boil disperses it. A džezva with no foam was either boiled too hard or left too long.

Instant Coffee & Variations

Standard Instant (Nescafé / Jacobs)
2 min · Office standard · Functional
  • Ratio: 1.5–2 teaspoons per 200ml water. Most people under-dose instant coffee, producing a watery result that tastes of neither coffee nor comfort.
  • Water temperature: 90°C — just off the boil. Fully boiling water scalds instant coffee and produces bitterness. Allow the kettle to sit for 30–45 seconds after boiling.
  • Method: Add coffee to the cup first. Pour a small amount of water (2 tbsp) and stir vigorously to dissolve completely before adding the remaining water. This prevents dry granules floating on the surface.
  • Honest assessment: Instant coffee will never match a properly brewed cup. However, a correctly made instant coffee is considerably better than an incorrectly made one — and better than most bad café coffee. It is the tool for mornings when the džezva is not an option.
Dalgona Coffee (Whipped Instant)
5 min · Visual · Works for a change
  • Ingredients: 2 tbsp instant coffee, 2 tbsp sugar, 2 tbsp hot water, 200ml cold milk or warm milk
  • Method: Combine instant coffee, sugar, and hot water in a bowl. Whisk vigorously by hand (3–5 minutes) or with a hand mixer (1–2 minutes) until the mixture becomes thick, pale brown foam — approximately tripled in volume. Pour milk into a glass. Spoon the whipped coffee on top. Stir before drinking to incorporate.
  • Note: The whipping process creates a temporary foam stable enough to float on milk. The result is substantially richer and more pleasant than the same instant coffee dissolved directly. Worth knowing as a variation on an uninspiring ingredient.
Instant Iced Coffee
3 min · Summer · The office version
  • Ingredients: 2 tsp instant coffee, 2 tsp sugar, 1 tbsp hot water, 150ml cold milk, ice
  • Method: Dissolve coffee and sugar in the hot water first — forms a syrupy concentrate. Fill a glass with ice, add cold milk, pour the concentrate over. Stir. The hot-water dissolving step prevents the coffee from remaining as undissolved granules in cold liquid.
  • Variation — Špricer od Kafe: Dissolve 1.5 tsp instant coffee in a small amount of hot water. Add cold sparkling water over ice. No milk. The carbonation provides texture in place of creaminess. Refreshing, lower calorie, and distinctly Bosnian-café in character.

Tea

Tea is underused in this household compared to its utility. It covers hydration with flavour when water feels insufficient, provides a caffeine-free wind-down option in the evening, and offers a genuinely large range of taste profiles within a category that costs almost nothing per cup. The following covers both bag tea (practical, everyday) and loose leaf (for those who want the full experience).

Temperature by Type

Green tea: 70–80°C (boiling water makes it bitter — let it cool for 3–4 minutes). Black tea: 95–100°C (near-boiling is correct here). Herbal / tisane: 100°C (full boil, these are robust). White tea: 75–85°C. Getting the temperature wrong is the most common reason tea tastes unpleasant.

Steeping Time

Black tea: 3–4 minutes. Green tea: 2–3 minutes maximum (longer produces astringency). Herbal: 5–7 minutes. White tea: 3–5 minutes. Over-steeping any tea increases tannin extraction, which produces bitterness regardless of tea quality. Set a timer — it costs nothing and produces a measurably better result.

Sarajevo Sourcing

Herbata, Franck, and Bi-Activ herbal teas are available everywhere. Lipton and Ahmad Tea bags at Konzum and Interspar for standard black tea. Larger Konzum and health shops (apoteke) carry chamomile, mint (menta), linden (lipa), and rosehip (šipak) in loose or bag form — all Bosnian staples with genuine medicinal history in this region.

Black Tea

🫖 English Breakfast / Ceylon

The standard morning black tea. Strong, malty, pairs with milk. Brew at 95–100°C, 3–4 minutes, remove the bag. Add a small amount of whole milk if desired — the milk reduces astringency and adds creaminess without sweetening. Sugar is personal choice. Ahmad Tea English Breakfast is available at Interspar and produces a genuinely good cup from a bag.

🍋 Earl Grey

Black tea with bergamot oil. Distinctive floral-citrus aroma. Works with or without milk. Brew identically to standard black tea but reduce steeping time slightly (2.5–3 minutes) — the bergamot amplifies bitterness with over-extraction. Best in the mid-morning as an alternative to a second coffee. Widely available in Sarajevo shops.

🌿 Loose-Leaf Black Tea

If transitioning from bags to loose leaf: buy a simple metal infuser (dm or a kitchenware shop, under 5 BAM). Ratio: 1 rounded teaspoon per 200ml. The difference in flavour between fresh loose-leaf tea and a tea bag that has been sitting in a box for six months is significant enough to justify the minor additional effort.

Green & White Tea

🍵 Green Tea (Sencha / Gunpowder)

The category most people ruin with boiling water. 75–80°C, 2–3 minutes maximum. The result should be pale gold to light green and taste grassy, clean, and slightly sweet — never bitter. Contains approximately one-third the caffeine of black tea, making it appropriate for mid-afternoon without disrupting evening sleep. Available at larger supermarkets and health shops.

🌸 Jasmine Green Tea

Green tea scented with jasmine blossoms. The most approachable entry point for those who find plain green tea too bitter or unfamiliar. The jasmine softens the profile considerably. Brew identically to standard green tea — the scenting does not change the temperature or timing requirements. Available in most supermarkets as bags.

☁️ White Tea

The least processed form of tea — delicate, lightly sweet, very low caffeine. 75–85°C, 3–5 minutes. The most appropriate tea for late evening if some warmth is wanted but caffeine must be minimised. More expensive than black or green tea — treat it as an occasion tea rather than an everyday one.

Herbal Teas & Tisanes

🌼 Chamomile (Kamilica)

The sleep-supporting herbal tea with the most consistent evidence behind it. Apigenin, an antioxidant in chamomile, binds to the same receptors as mild sedatives. 100°C, 5–7 minutes, covered while steeping to retain volatile compounds. With honey. Consumed 30–45 minutes before sleep as part of the evening protocol. Available in every apoteka and supermarket in Bosnia.

🌿 Peppermint (Menta)

Caffeine-free, digestively supportive, and distinctively refreshing both hot and cold. 100°C, 5 minutes. Good after a heavy meal — menthol relaxes the smooth muscle of the digestive tract, reducing post-meal discomfort. Also genuinely effective as a mild headache remedy when drunk hot during the onset of tension headaches. Grows in this region — buy fresh or dried.

🌺 Rosehip (Šipak)

Exceptionally high in Vitamin C — significantly more per gram than citrus fruit. Tart, fruity, naturally sweet. 100°C, 7–10 minutes for dried rosehips (longer extraction needed). Particularly useful during winter for immune support. Bosnian šipak tea is a regional staple — buy dried rosehips loose from a pijaca or apoteka. Make a large pot and refrigerate — it is also excellent cold.

🌲 Linden Flower (Lipa)

Bosnian traditional tea for calming anxiety and mild fever. Mild honey-floral flavour, entirely caffeine-free. 95°C, 5 minutes. Combined with chamomile for the strongest sleep-supporting herbal combination available without a prescription. The linden tree is a Sarajevo symbol — the tea from its flowers has been consumed here for centuries.

🫚 Ginger Tea (Đumbir)

Fresh ginger root, sliced thin: 4–5 slices per 300ml water. Simmer for 10 minutes (do not just steep — ginger requires heat to release its active compounds). Strain, add lemon juice and honey. Anti-inflammatory, digestively warming, and genuinely effective against nausea. Use fresh root — dried or powder versions lose most of the volatile oils that give fresh ginger its character.

🍋 Lemon & Honey

Not a tea in the botanical sense — hot water, fresh lemon juice, honey. The simplest soothing hot drink available. At the onset of a cold: add a small grated piece of fresh ginger and a pinch of turmeric. The combination is more effective than any packaged cold remedy containing the same compounds in smaller quantities at greater expense.

Sarajevo winters are cold enough to justify drinks that warm deliberately — not just incidentally. These are prepared hot, some mulled, some spiced, all non-alcoholic. Spiced Apple Cider (Hot)

Bosnian Boza
Traditional · Thick, sweet, warming

Turmeric Milk (Golden Milk)
10 min · Anti-inflammatory · Pre-sleep or post-workout

Daily Drink Protocol

  • Upon waking: Water — 300–400ml, room temperature or cold, before coffee. The body is dehydrated after 7–8 hours without fluid. Coffee on an empty, dehydrated stomach increases cortisol beyond the natural morning peak and can cause stomach discomfort. Water first is a two-minute habit with measurable impact on morning energy.
  • First coffee: 90 minutes after waking. Cortisol is naturally elevated for the first 60–90 minutes after waking (the cortisol awakening response). Caffeine during this window adds to an already elevated state and develops tolerance faster. Waiting until cortisol begins its natural decline makes the coffee meaningfully more effective and extends its useful range through the day.
  • Cut caffeine at 14:00. Caffeine's half-life is 5–6 hours. A 14:00 coffee still has 50% of its caffeine active at 19:00–20:00. For an 22:30–23:00 sleep target, the cut-off is not conservative — it is mathematically correct.
  • Herbal tea in the evening. Chamomile, linden, or lemon-honey after 20:00. Signals the wind-down. No preparation required for a tea bag and hot water. The ritual matters as much as the compound.
  • Water target: 2–2.5 litres on sedentary days, more on training days. Achieved through consistent sipping rather than large infrequent quantities. Pale yellow urine throughout the day is the practical measurement.

Baking Principles

Weigh, Don't Measure

A cup of flour can vary by 30% depending on how it was scooped. A cup of sugar is similarly imprecise. Use a scale. This is not pedantry — it is the difference between a recipe working and not working.

Room Temperature Ingredients

Butter, eggs, and dairy should be at room temperature before combining unless explicitly stated otherwise. Cold butter does not cream. Cold eggs cause chocolate to seize. Plan 30–60 minutes ahead.

Do Not Overmix

Once flour meets wet ingredients, gluten begins developing. Overmixing produces tough, chewy baked goods. Mix until just combined and no more. A few streaks of flour visible when you stop is acceptable.

Oven Calibration

Know your oven. The first time you use a new recipe, check progress 5 minutes early. If things consistently burn or undercook, your oven temperature is off. Adjust accordingly and note it for future reference.

Butter Croissants (Two-Day Method)

  • Dough ingredients: 500g strong white flour, 10g salt, 80g sugar, 7g instant yeast, 300ml whole milk (warm), 30g butter (softened)
  • Butter block: 250g cold unsalted butter (high fat content — at least 82% — Lurpak or similar)
  • Day 1 — Dough: Combine flour, salt, sugar, yeast. Add warm milk gradually. Knead 8–10 minutes until smooth and elastic. Add softened butter, knead another 5 minutes. The dough should be smooth and slightly tacky. Wrap and refrigerate overnight.
  • Day 1 — Butter block: Pound cold butter between two sheets of parchment into a 18x18cm square. Refrigerate alongside the dough.
  • Day 2 — Lamination: Roll dough into a 38x20cm rectangle. Place butter block in the centre. Fold dough over butter like an envelope, sealing the edges. Roll out to 60x20cm. Fold into thirds (like a letter). Wrap, refrigerate 30 minutes. Repeat this roll-and-fold twice more with 30-minute rests between each. Three folds total — this creates 27 layers.
  • Shaping: Roll dough to 4mm thickness. Cut into triangles with a 10cm base. Roll each triangle from the base to the tip with gentle tension, stretching slightly as you roll. Curve the ends slightly inward.
  • Proof: Place on lined baking sheet. Cover loosely, room temperature, 2–3 hours until visibly puffed and jiggling when the tray is shaken.
  • Bake: Brush with egg wash (1 egg, 1 tbsp milk). 190°C for 15–18 minutes until deep amber. Not golden — amber. The colour is flavour.
Pain au Chocolat
Use croissant dough above · 12 pieces
  • Method: Roll the laminated croissant dough to 4mm thickness. Cut into 10x15cm rectangles. Place 2 pieces of good dark chocolate (70%) near one short edge. Roll once to cover the chocolate, place another piece of chocolate, roll fully. Seal the edge underneath.
  • Proof and bake: Exactly as croissants. The chocolate should be in bars (not chips) so it stays in place during proofing and melts cleanly during baking.
  • Chocolate quality: Lindt 70% from dm or Interspar. The quality of the chocolate is entirely visible in the finished product. Do not substitute milk chocolate — the bitterness of dark chocolate is what balances the buttery pastry.
Shortcrust Pastry (Base Recipe)
30 min + chill · Base for tarts and quiches
  • Ingredients: 200g plain flour, 100g cold butter (cubed), 1 egg yolk, 2–3 tbsp ice cold water, pinch of salt, (for sweet tarts: add 30g icing sugar)
  • Method: Rub cold butter into flour with fingertips until it resembles breadcrumbs. Keep everything cold — warm hands melt the butter. Add egg yolk and cold water one tablespoon at a time, mixing with a fork until dough just comes together. Do not knead.
  • Chill: Flatten into a disc, wrap, refrigerate 30 minutes minimum. This relaxes the gluten and firms the butter, both of which are essential for a short (crumbly, not tough) result.
  • Blind bake: For filled tarts: line tin, prick base with fork, fill with parchment and baking beans (or rice). Bake 180°C for 15 minutes, remove beans, bake 5 more minutes until pale gold. This prevents a soggy base.

Cakes

Classic Victoria Sponge
45 min · 8–10 slices · The foundational cake
  • Ingredients: 200g unsalted butter (room temperature), 200g caster sugar, 4 eggs (room temperature), 200g self-raising flour (or plain flour + 2 tsp baking powder), 1 tsp vanilla extract, pinch of salt
  • Filling: 150ml double cream (whipped), 3 tbsp good quality strawberry jam
  • Cream butter and sugar: Beat together for 4–5 minutes until very pale, almost white, and noticeably increased in volume. This is not a brief stir — the air incorporated here is what makes the cake rise. This step cannot be rushed.
  • Add eggs: One at a time, beating well after each. If the mixture curdles (looks split), add a tablespoon of flour and continue — it will come back together.
  • Fold flour: Sift flour over the batter, fold with a large spoon in large, gentle strokes. Do not beat. Count strokes if necessary — approximately 20 is enough. Stop when just combined.
  • Bake: Divide between two greased and lined 20cm round tins. 180°C for 20–25 minutes until a skewer comes out clean and edges pull from the tin. Cool completely before filling — warm cake melts cream.
  • Assemble: Spread jam on one layer, whipped cream on top of jam, sandwich with second layer. Dust with icing sugar.
Chocolate Ganache Cake
1 hour + cooling · 10–12 slices · For occasions
  • Cake ingredients: 200g plain flour, 200g caster sugar, 75g good cocoa powder, 1.5 tsp baking soda, 1 tsp salt, 2 eggs, 240ml buttermilk (or milk + 1 tbsp vinegar, rested 10 min), 240ml hot coffee (intensifies chocolate flavour — invisible in the final taste), 120ml neutral oil, 2 tsp vanilla
  • Ganache: 200g dark chocolate (70%), 200ml double cream, 20g butter
  • Mix: Combine dry ingredients. Combine wet ingredients separately. Mix wet into dry — this is a loose batter, not a thick one. That is correct. Do not add more flour.
  • Bake: Two 20cm round tins, 175°C for 30–35 minutes. This cake is moist and slightly fragile when warm — do not attempt to remove from tins for at least 15 minutes.
  • Ganache: Bring cream to just below boiling. Pour over finely chopped chocolate. Wait 2 minutes, then stir from the centre outward until completely smooth. Add butter, stir in. Allow to cool at room temperature until spreadable — approximately 1 hour. Do not refrigerate to speed this up: it causes grainy texture.
  • Assemble: Level cake tops with a serrated knife if necessary. Spread ganache between layers and over the exterior. The finish does not need to be perfect — a palette knife pulled through at an angle creates a deliberate textured effect.
Lemon Drizzle Cake
50 min · 8–10 slices · Loaf tin
  • Ingredients: 175g butter (room temperature), 175g caster sugar, 3 eggs, 175g self-raising flour, zest of 2 unwaxed lemons, 2 tbsp milk
  • Drizzle: Juice of 2 lemons (approximately 60ml), 75g caster sugar
  • Method: Cream butter and sugar until pale and fluffy, 4 minutes. Add eggs one at a time. Fold in flour, lemon zest, and milk. Pour into greased and lined loaf tin.
  • Bake: 180°C for 40–45 minutes until golden and a skewer comes out clean.
  • Drizzle: While cake is still warm and in the tin, mix lemon juice and sugar (do not heat). Pierce the cake all over with a skewer. Pour the drizzle slowly over the top, allowing it to soak in. The sugar forms a delicate crust as it dries. Do not ice or dust with icing sugar — the drizzle is the finish.

Biscuits & Small Bakes

Brown Butter Chocolate Chip Cookies
30 min + 1hr chill · 18–20 cookies · The definitive home cookie
  • Ingredients: 170g unsalted butter, 200g plain flour, ½ tsp baking soda, 1 tsp salt, 150g soft brown sugar, 100g caster sugar, 1 egg + 1 egg yolk (room temperature), 2 tsp vanilla extract, 200g dark chocolate (roughly chopped — not chips)
  • Brown butter: Melt butter in a light-coloured pan over medium heat. Continue cooking, swirling occasionally, until the foam subsides and golden-brown specks appear at the bottom and the smell becomes nutty. Immediately pour into a bowl — it burns in seconds once it reaches this point. Allow to cool 10 minutes.
  • Mix: Whisk brown butter with both sugars, 1 minute. Add egg, yolk, and vanilla — whisk vigorously for 2 minutes until the mixture thickens and becomes glossy. This step builds structure.
  • Fold: Add flour, salt, baking soda — fold until just combined. Fold in chocolate chunks.
  • Chill: Refrigerate dough for at least 1 hour — overnight is better. Chilling deepens flavour and controls spread.
  • Bake: Scoop onto lined baking sheet, spacing generously. 175°C for 11–13 minutes — edges set, centre appears underdone. This is correct. They firm as they cool. Remove at this point or they will be hard by the next day.
Shortbread
30 min · 20–24 pieces · Three ingredients done correctly
  • Ingredients: 250g cold unsalted butter (cubed), 125g icing sugar (sifted), 375g plain flour, pinch of salt, optional: 1 tsp vanilla or zest of 1 lemon
  • Method: In a food processor or by hand, rub butter into flour and icing sugar until the mixture just comes together into a dough. Do not overwork. Flatten into a disc, wrap, chill 30 minutes.
  • Shape: Roll to 1cm thickness. Cut into rectangles or rounds. Prick each piece twice with a fork — this is traditional and allows steam to escape, keeping them flat.
  • Bake: 160°C for 18–22 minutes until just barely golden at the edges. Pale is correct — over-baking produces a hard, bitter result. Shortbread should crumble at the bite, not snap.
  • Cool: Leave on the tray for 10 minutes before moving — they are extremely fragile when warm.

Desserts

Crème Brûlée
1 hour + overnight chill · 4 servings · Impressive, achievable
  • Ingredients: 500ml double cream, 1 vanilla pod (or 2 tsp vanilla extract), 5 egg yolks, 100g caster sugar, plus 4 tbsp caster sugar for the topping
  • Infuse: Heat cream with split vanilla pod until just simmering. Remove from heat, allow to infuse 15 minutes.
  • Custard: Whisk egg yolks with sugar until pale. Remove vanilla pod, slowly pour warm cream into yolk mixture while whisking constantly. Pour slowly — adding too fast scrambles the yolks.
  • Strain and pour: Strain through a fine sieve into a jug. Pour into 4 ramekins. Place ramekins in a deep baking dish. Pour hot water into the dish to come halfway up the ramekins — this water bath ensures even, gentle cooking.
  • Bake: 150°C for 35–40 minutes until just set with a slight wobble in the centre. Cool to room temperature, then refrigerate overnight. The overnight rest is important for texture.
  • Brûlée: Sprinkle 1 tbsp caster sugar evenly over each custard. Torch in circular motion, keeping the flame moving until the sugar has caramelised completely — amber, not black. Serve within 5 minutes of torching while the shell is still crisp.
Tiramisu
30 min + 4hr chill · 8–10 servings · No baking required
  • Ingredients: 6 egg yolks, 150g caster sugar, 500g mascarpone (room temperature), 300ml double cream, 300ml strong espresso or very strong coffee (cooled), 3 tbsp coffee liqueur (optional), approximately 30 savoiardi / ladyfinger biscuits, cocoa powder for dusting
  • Zabaglione: Whisk egg yolks and sugar over a pot of barely simmering water (do not let the bowl touch the water) for 8–10 minutes until pale, thick, and tripled in volume. The mixture should hold a ribbon when the whisk is lifted. Remove from heat, continue whisking until cooled — approximately 5 minutes.
  • Fold: Add mascarpone to the cooled zabaglione, fold gently until smooth. In a separate bowl, whip double cream to soft peaks. Fold into mascarpone mixture in two additions.
  • Assemble: Briefly dip each ladyfinger in coffee (one second per side — not soaked, just moistened; a saturated biscuit collapses). Layer in dish, cover with half the cream. Second layer of dipped biscuits, cover with remaining cream.
  • Chill: Refrigerate at least 4 hours, overnight preferred. Dust generously with good cocoa powder only when serving.
  • Mascarpone: Available at larger Interspar and Konzum stores. If unavailable, full-fat cream cheese loosened with 2 tbsp cream is an acceptable substitute.
Baklava (Bosnian Style)
1.5 hours · 20–24 pieces · Regional and correct
  • Ingredients: 500g phyllo/yufka pastry, 200g unsalted butter (melted), 300g walnuts (finely chopped — not ground), 2 tbsp sugar, 1 tsp cinnamon
  • Syrup: 300g sugar, 250ml water, 1 tbsp lemon juice, optional: 1 tbsp rose water
  • Filling: Mix chopped walnuts with sugar and cinnamon. The walnut pieces should have texture — not paste.
  • Layer: Brush a 30x20cm baking tin with butter. Layer 6 sheets of phyllo, brushing each with melted butter. Spread a thin, even layer of walnut filling. Add 2–3 more buttered phyllo sheets. Another layer of filling. Continue until filling is used, finishing with 6–8 layers of buttered phyllo on top.
  • Cut: Before baking, cut the entire tray into diamond shapes with a sharp knife — cut all the way through. This is essential — baked baklava cannot be cut cleanly without shattering.
  • Bake: 170°C for 35–40 minutes until deep golden.
  • Syrup: While baklava bakes, bring sugar and water to a boil, add lemon juice, simmer 10 minutes. Add rose water off heat. The syrup must be hot when the baklava comes out, or at room temperature when poured onto completely cold baklava — never the same temperature. Hot syrup on hot baklava = soggy. Cold syrup on cold baklava = no absorption.
  • Rest: 2 hours minimum before serving. The syrup needs time to penetrate all layers.
Chocolate Fondant (Lava Cake)
30 min · 4 servings · Restaurant result at home
  • Ingredients: 100g dark chocolate (70%), 100g unsalted butter, 2 eggs, 2 egg yolks, 100g caster sugar, 50g plain flour, pinch of salt, cocoa powder for dusting ramekins
  • Prep: Butter 4 ramekins well, dust with cocoa powder, tap out excess. This step prevents sticking and is the only insurance against a fondant that does not unmould cleanly.
  • Ganache base: Melt chocolate and butter together over a bain-marie or in microwave in 30-second bursts, stirring between. Allow to cool 5 minutes.
  • Mix: Whisk eggs, yolks, and sugar until pale and slightly thickened, 2 minutes. Fold in chocolate mixture. Fold in flour and salt until just combined.
  • Chill: Fill ramekins three-quarters full. At this point the fondants can be refrigerated for up to 24 hours — an exceptional advantage for preparing ahead when serving guests.
  • Bake: 200°C for exactly 12 minutes from room temperature (14 minutes if baking from cold). The edges should be set, the centre still soft. Rest in ramekin 1 minute. Run a knife around the edge, invert onto a plate, hold 10 seconds, lift the ramekin. Serve immediately with vanilla ice cream or cream.

The Bag Itself

Size

15–20 litres. Enough to carry what matters. Small enough to remain unobtrusive and to fit under an aircraft seat. A bag that requires its own announcement upon entering a room is too large.

Material

Waxed canvas in black or charcoal (weathers well, looks better with age), or ballistic nylon (lighter, more water-resistant). No glossy materials. Nothing that requires special care. In Sarajevo's wet winters, water resistance is a practical requirement, not a luxury.

Packing Rules

  • Pack the bag the evening prior. Never in the morning. The morning has enough friction without adding the mental load of assembling the bag under time pressure.
  • Assign everything a fixed place. The laptop is always in the sleeve. The medications are always in the front right pocket. The notebook is always in the front left. Fixed placement means retrieval without searching.
  • The bag returns empty of temporary items, stocked with permanent ones. Receipts, used tissues, and empty packaging are removed each evening. The permanent items — medications, grooming pouch, adapters — are always there and never removed.

The Notification Audit — First Principles

Before discussing any specific app, the most important intervention available on a modern smartphone: turn off every notification, then deliberately re-enable only the ones that genuinely require immediate response. Most people do the reverse — accept all notifications by default and disable the ones that become intolerable. The result is a device that interrupts constantly.

  • Immediate response required (allow): Phone calls from people who matter. Direct messages from close contacts. Calendar alerts for imminent events. That is likely a complete list for most people.
  • Batched — check on your schedule (silent / badge only): Email. Messaging apps. Social media. News. These do not require interruption — they reward scheduled attention.
  • Never (disable entirely): Every app that delivers content rather than communication. YouTube, Instagram, LinkedIn, browser, weather. These apps have no interest in your time. You decide when to engage with them.
  • On Android: Settings → Notifications → App notifications. Review each app individually. Twenty minutes once, daily returns indefinitely.

Android Customisation — Beyond the Launcher

Always-On Display & Lock Screen

  • Always-On Display: Settings → Lock screen → Always On Display: On. Shows time, date, and notification icons without waking the full screen. Eliminates the habit of unlocking the phone to check the time — which reliably leads to checking notifications, which leads to thirty minutes disappearing.
  • Lock screen shortcuts: Remove social media shortcuts from the lock screen. Replace with Torch and Camera if your device allows it — or remove shortcuts entirely. The lock screen should provide information, not temptation.
  • Lock screen widgets: On supported Android versions (14+), add a minimal clock widget and battery percentage only. Nothing that invites scrolling before the day has properly begun.

System Font & Display

  • Font size: Settings → Display → Font size and style. Increase by one step from default. Eye strain over a full day of reading on a small screen is real — larger text is not vanity, it is visual economy.
  • Display size: Settings → Display → Display size. Slightly smaller than default on large-screen phones creates a cleaner, less crowded interface. Test at one step below default.
  • System font (Samsung / MIUI): Settings → Display → Font style. Samsung devices allow font changes without third-party apps. Install "Samsung One UI Sans" or "One UI 6" font styles for a more refined interface. On stock Android, this requires a launcher that supports font changes (Niagara does not — Lawnchair 2 does).
  • Colour profile: Settings → Display → Screen mode → Natural or AMOLED Cinema. Avoid the default "Vivid" — it over-saturates colours in a way that looks artificial in direct comparison with physical objects. Natural is accurate; AMOLED Cinema is a slight enhancement without distortion.

Battery & Performance

  • Adaptive Battery: On. Settings → Battery → Adaptive Battery. Learns your usage patterns and restricts background activity for rarely used apps. More effective than manually restricting individual apps because it operates on actual usage data.
  • Background app refresh: Settings → Battery → Background usage limits → Restrict background usage for every app that does not need to push real-time data. Social media apps do not need background access — they can refresh when you open them.
  • Charge limit at 80% (if device supports it): Settings → Battery → Protect Battery. Stops charging at 80% to extend long-term battery health. The full 80–100% range degrades lithium-ion cells significantly faster than the 20–80% range. On a phone you intend to keep for 3+ years, this is meaningful.
  • Dark mode system-wide: Settings → Display → Dark: On. On AMOLED screens (most Samsung, most flagship Android), dark mode meaningfully extends battery life because black pixels on AMOLED are literally powered off. Not merely dim — off.

  • Photos: reviewed weekly. Five minutes Sunday — remove duplicates, temporary screenshots, blurred photos. A library of 40,000 images contains no more accessible memories than 4,000 curated ones. The curation is the act of deciding what mattered.
  • Downloads folder: cleared monthly. A transit point, not storage. Every file is either moved to its correct location or deleted. Nothing stays in Downloads beyond one week.
  • Contacts: maintained annually. Remove entries with no name, no longer relevant, or duplicated. A contact list that cannot be navigated is not a contact list — it is a list.

The Principle of Intentional Listening

Most people treat music as ambience — something playing in the background that was never consciously chosen. This is a wasted resource. Music affects heart rate, cortisol levels, dopamine release, and the rate at which the prefrontal cortex processes information. Understanding what each type of music does physiologically allows you to deploy it with intention rather than habit.

The most important rule: music with lyrics competes directly with language-processing tasks. Reading, writing, analysing, and coding all use the same neural pathways as verbal processing. Lyrical music fought for in that space produces measurably worse output on language tasks than instrumental music or silence. Save lyrical music for physical tasks, commuting, and chores — where the verbal processing centre is not occupied by anything else.

Volume Principle

Low enough to stop noticing it is playing — that is the correct volume for focus work. Loud enough to feel it in the body — that is the correct volume for physical tasks. The transition between these two settings is the transition between mental and physical engagement.

Lyrical vs Instrumental

Instrumental for: deep work, reading, writing, analysis. Lyrical for: training, chores, cleaning, commuting, cooking. The rule is simple because the neuroscience is simple: two verbal streams cannot fully occupy the same channel simultaneously.

Tempo & Energy

BPM (beats per minute) influences heart rate and therefore arousal state. 60–80 BPM for focused calm work. 100–130 BPM for sustained physical effort. 130–160+ BPM for high-intensity training. Match the tempo to the physiological state you need, not the one you are already in.

Deep Focus — Work & Study

The goal during focused analytical work is to occupy just enough of the auditory cortex to suppress environmental distraction, without competing with the language centres needed for reading and writing. Ambient, textural, and classical instrumental music achieves this. The brain habituates to it within minutes, after which it functions as a cognitive shield rather than an additional input.

🎹 Hans Zimmer — Study & Work

Cinematic orchestral without lyrics. Interstellar, Inception, and Gladiator soundtracks contain extended tracks at 60–80 BPM ideal for sustained analytical work. The harmonic language is rich enough to be interesting but never demanding enough to pull attention away from the task. Search: "Hans Zimmer study music" on Spotify.

🎵 Lofi Hip-Hop

The genre was engineered for exactly this use case. Slow BPM (70–90), no lyrics, gentle percussion, warm analogue textures. "Lofi Girl" on Spotify and YouTube is the canonical source. The slight imperfections in the production — the vinyl crackle, the slightly off-beat hi-hats — prevent the brain from fully habituating to silence, which keeps alertness marginally elevated without stimulation.

🎻 Chopin Nocturnes / Bach Cello Suites

For those who find modern ambient music texturally thin: Chopin's Nocturnes (Op.9, Op.15, Op.27) are among the most focus-compatible pieces ever written — melodically present but emotionally unhurried. Bach's Cello Suites in G Major are similarly calibrated. Both at low volume. Both on every streaming platform.

🌧️ Rain & Brown Noise

Not music — but a legitimate auditory focus tool. Brown noise (deeper than white noise) has the strongest research support for masking distraction and improving sustained attention. Rain sounds achieve a similar effect through natural familiarity. "Rain on Window" or "Brown Noise" on Spotify or YouTube. For open-plan offices or environments with irregular noise patterns.

🎷 Jazz — Miles Davis / Coltrane

Kind of Blue by Miles Davis (1959) is the most-streamed jazz album for a reason: it operates at exactly the threshold of interesting-without-demanding. For focused work, Kind of Blue, Bill Evans' Waltz for Debby, or any Coltrane ballads album. Avoid bebop (too rhythmically complex, too demanding) — the slow modal jazz is the target.

How to Watch YouTube Without Being Consumed By It

YouTube's recommendation algorithm is designed to maximise time on platform, not to maximise the value of that time. Left unmanaged, it will route you from a useful video on fitness into reaction content, entertainment, and algorithmically optimised noise within three clicks. The channels above are curated — but the platform they sit on is not your ally.

  • Subscribe deliberately, not reflexively. Subscribe only to channels you intend to return to. A subscription list of 200 channels is not a list — it is another inbox that demands management. Fewer than 20 channels, carefully chosen, is a better collection than a hundred accumulated by impulse.
  • Use playlists as your queue. When you find a video worth watching, add it to a "Watch Later" playlist rather than watching immediately. This separates discovery from consumption and prevents the session from extending indefinitely because one video ends and another begins automatically.
  • Disable Autoplay. Settings → Autoplay → Off. This is the single most important YouTube configuration change available. Autoplay is the algorithm's primary mechanism for extending your session beyond your intention.
  • Use YouTube on the television, not the phone. The television is a deliberate consumption device — you sit down to watch something specific and you leave when it is done. The phone is a frictionless device that enables passive consumption anywhere. Watching YouTube on a large screen at a scheduled time is categorically different from scrolling it on a phone in bed.
  • The browser extension approach: On desktop, the uBlock Origin extension (already in the Windows 11 tab's app recommendations) combined with the "Unhook" YouTube extension removes the homepage feed, suggested videos, and comments — leaving only the search function and your subscriptions. This transforms YouTube from a recommendation engine into a library you query intentionally.

How to Vet a Temu Listing — The Protocol

Every category below comes with search terms. Before purchasing anything, apply this vetting sequence. It takes under three minutes and eliminates the majority of poor purchases.

01

Check review count and recency

Fewer than 50 reviews means insufficient data — proceed with caution. Reviews older than 6 months on a Temu listing may reflect a different product batch from a different supplier. Check buyer-submitted photos

The listing photos are optimised by the seller. The buyer photos are what the product actually looks like in a normal environment. The Core Principle — Cost Per Use

The correct metric for evaluating any purchase is not the price. It is the cost per use. A €10 item used once costs €10 per use. A €60 item used 400 times costs €0.15 per use. Evaluating purchases through this lens eliminates the majority of poor buying decisions instantly.

Apply the formula before any non-trivial purchase: Cost ÷ Expected uses = Cost per use. A €45 pair of leather shoes worn 300 times costs 15 cents per wear. A €15 pair worn 20 times before they disintegrate costs 75 cents per wear. The €45 shoes are the cheaper purchase by a factor of five.

The Buying Rules — Applied Decision-Making

01

The 48-Hour Rule on non-essential purchases

Any non-essential purchase above €20: wait 48 hours before completing it. The impulse to buy is strongest at the point of discovery. Most impulse purchases fail the 48-hour test — the desire diminishes significantly once separated from the trigger (an advertisement, a recommendation, a moment of browsing). If you still want it after 48 hours, the purchase is more likely to reflect genuine need.

02

Buy for the life you have, not the life you plan

The most common poor purchase is buying for a projected future use that never materialises. The camping equipment for a trip you have not booked. The cooking equipment for a skill you have not developed. Purchase what serves the current reality. If the planned activity becomes a consistent habit, upgrade then — on evidence, not aspiration.

03

One in, one out

Before purchasing any new item in a category where you already own an equivalent, identify what you will remove when the new item arrives. This prevents accumulation and forces honest evaluation of whether the new item is genuinely better than the current one or merely new. If you cannot identify what to remove, you do not need the new item.

04

Read the negative reviews on any purchase over €30

Positive reviews confirm what a product does well — which is already stated in the listing. Negative reviews identify the failure modes — which are not. A product with 4.5 stars and 200 reviews where the negative reviews consistently mention "hinge broke after 3 months" has a design flaw that the star rating obscures. Read the negatives before committing.

05

Avoid sales psychology traps

"Limited time offer," "only 3 left," "60% off" — these are mechanisms designed to trigger purchase before rational evaluation. A 60% discount on an item you would not have bought at full price is not a saving. It is a €12 loss dressed as a €28 gain. The correct question is always: would I buy this at full price? If no, the discount changes nothing about whether you need it.

06

Seasonal shopping calendar

Certain items have predictable discount cycles. Winter coats: January–February after the season ends (30–50% off remaining stock). Summer clothing: August. Electronics: Black Friday in November, though the discounts in BiH are less dramatic than in Western Europe. Knowing when to buy produces the same quality at 20–40% less cost with no sacrifice in selection.

07

The maintenance premium — factor it in

The purchase price is not the full cost of ownership. Leather shoes require polish and cedar shoe trees (add ~20 BAM per pair per year). A wool overcoat requires dry cleaning (add 20–30 BAM per season). A quality watch requires servicing every 3–5 years. This does not make these purchases wrong — it makes the true cost calculation accurate. Buy the item only when you can also sustain the maintenance.

Understanding Sarajevo

Sarajevo is one of the few cities in the world where you can stand at a single intersection and see a mosque, an Orthodox church, a Catholic cathedral, and a Sephardic synagogue within the same city block. This is not an accident — it is the legacy of the Ottoman millet system, which administered different religious communities under the same umbrella with considerable local autonomy. The city at its best still functions this way: multiple identities coexisting in a small, navigable space.

Understanding this history does not require formal study. Walk slowly through Baščaršija. Notice the architecture changing as you move west from the Ottoman old town into the Austro-Hungarian grid of the 19th century, then into the Socialist modernism of the mid-20th century, then into the contemporary construction at the edges. The city is a physical timeline. You are walking through it every day.

Communication Architecture

📊

The Problem Brief

When reporting on any issue, in exactly this order: (1) What is the problem, in one sentence. (2) What is the cause, if known. (3) What is the current status. (4) What action is being taken. (5) Timeline for resolution. This structure works verbally and in writing. It prevents the common alternative: a narrative that begins with background, adds detail, and only reveals the actual problem in the fifth sentence, by which time the listener has begun solving the wrong problem.

🎯

One Ask Per Communication

An email or message with three questions receives an answer to one — often not the most important one. When multiple things are needed: three separate short messages with a single clear request each, or a structured numbered list in one message where each item is visually and conceptually separate. Clarity is a courtesy. Vague requests transfer the burden of interpretation to the recipient.

⏱️

Commitments with Timelines

"I'll handle it" offers zero information. "I'll have that resolved by 3pm today and will send confirmation when done" is a commitment that can be tracked, relied upon, and builds trust with every instance of delivery. Over time, the professional who consistently delivers precisely what they promised becomes the professional others go to when something genuinely must be done.

📝

Documentation as Legacy

Your incident notes, runbooks, and ticket records are your professional legacy. A well-documented system can be maintained by someone else when you are unavailable. A poorly documented one cannot. The professional who documents well is never fully absent — their knowledge persists. This is also the fastest way to demonstrate depth of contribution to those who evaluate it.

Technical Communication — Explained Clearly

A significant part of your role is translating technical reality into language that non-technical stakeholders can act on. This is a skill entirely separate from technical ability and one that most technically talented people underinvest in.

What Not to Say

"The DHCP server is failing to allocate IP addresses due to scope exhaustion, and the DNS resolution is cascading as a result."

The Lycaon Translation

"The network cannot assign addresses to new devices because we've run out of available slots — it's like a hotel with no vacant rooms. I'm expanding capacity now and will have this resolved within 30 minutes. In the meantime, devices already connected will not be affected."

What Not to Say

"We've had a SQL injection vulnerability exposed through the login endpoint — need to patch the WAF rule and sanitise inputs."

The Lycaon Translation

"There's a security gap in the login system that could allow an attacker to access data they shouldn't. I'm closing it now — estimated 45 minutes. No evidence it's been exploited yet, but I'll confirm this in my report within 2 hours."

The analogy is the bridge. The specific technical term is replaced with something familiar. The conclusion (what is being done and when) is given before the technical detail, not after it. The listener's first and primary need is: is this being handled? Answer that first, always.

The Email Standard

  • Subject line: Specific enough to be retrieved in twelve months. Not "Question" — "VPN Access Request — [Name] — [Date]." Not "Issue" — "Resolved: Email Gateway Outage 04/03." A subject line is an index entry, not a teaser.
  • Three-to-five sentence body rule: For most professional emails, the entire message should be three to five sentences. State the purpose, provide the necessary context, make the ask or deliver the information, and close with a clear next step. If an email exceeds ten sentences, ask whether a conversation would serve better.
  • The passive voice removes accountability. "Mistakes were made" is the famous example. In professional email: "The system was configured incorrectly" vs "I configured the system incorrectly and here is how I have corrected it." Own your work — including its failures. This is unusual enough to build significant trust when done consistently.
  • Never send angry email. Write it. Save it as a draft. Read it in the morning. Send the version you would be comfortable with your senior leadership reading. The version written in frustration and the version that serves your professional interests are never the same document.
  • The signature: Name, role, contact number, and nothing else. No motivational quotes. No large graphic logos. No lists of qualifications. Professional brevity in a signature signals confidence, not modesty.

Handling Difficult Situations

The Frustrated User+

The error is to begin solving immediately. The correct sequence: acknowledge before you analyse. Two sentences: "I understand this has disrupted your work — that's genuinely frustrating and I'm going to prioritise getting this resolved for you right now." Then go technical. The problem will take the same time to fix either way. The difference is how they experience the interaction and how they describe it afterwards. "The IT guy fixed it" versus "he was incredibly helpful and had it sorted within the hour" are produced by different approaches to the same fix.

When You Don't Know the Answer+

The incorrect response: a guess, delivered with false confidence, that later proves wrong. The correct response: "I don't have the answer to that immediately, but I can find out. Give me [specific time] and I'll have a definitive answer for you." Then do exactly that. The ability to say "I don't know yet but I will" and then deliver on that commitment is genuinely rare and immediately distinguishing. No one expects omniscience. Everyone notices honesty followed by reliability.

Being Asked to Do Something Outside Your Role+

Two approaches depending on the request. If it is adjacent to your skills and the relationship merits it: do it, without complaint, and quietly note the precedent you have set. Versatility builds capital. If it is genuinely outside what you should be doing: "I want to help with this — I'm not sure I'm the right person, but I can either point you to who is, or discuss what support I'm actually in a position to offer. Which would be more useful?" This is neither refusal nor capitulation. It is clarity offered generously.

Taking Criticism Professionally+

The natural reaction to criticism is defence. The professional reaction is inquiry. "Thank you for raising this — can you help me understand specifically what you'd like to see done differently?" This disarms defensive exchanges, produces actionable feedback instead of vague criticism, and positions you as someone interested in improvement rather than someone interested in winning an argument. These responses produce entirely different professional reputations over time.

The Critical Incident — Incident Mode+

In a major outage or security incident: composure is the first deployment. The room will take its emotional cue from whoever is most visibly calm. If that person is you, the room settles. If you are visibly panicking, others panic and compound the dysfunction. Practical incident mode: state clearly what is known, what is unknown, what is being done, and what the communication cadence will be. "Here is what we know. Here is what we don't yet know. Here are the three actions being taken right now. I will update you in 30 minutes regardless of whether there is news." Then execute. The contrast between this composure and the chaos around it will be noticed and remembered.

Desktop Philosophy

  • Zero desktop icons — without exception. Every icon on the desktop is a decision deferred. All shortcuts belong in the Start Menu or Taskbar. The desktop is a viewing surface for your wallpaper, not a filing system.
  • Virtual desktops — use them. Win + Tab opens the virtual desktop manager. Create one for Work, one for Personal, one for a current project. Switch with Ctrl + Win + Arrow. The cognitive separation between contexts is genuine and worth the setup.
  • The taskbar is not a storage solution. Pin only what you use daily. Everything else opens from Start or a keyboard shortcut.

Taskbar & Start Menu

Taskbar Configuration

  • Alignment: Left. Settings → Personalisation → Taskbar → Alignment: Left. The brain reads left-to-right; a centred taskbar disrupts spatial consistency and muscle memory built over years of use.
  • Auto-hide: On. The taskbar appears on hover and disappears otherwise. Reclaims ~48px of screen — meaningful on a 1080p display when reading documents or code.
  • Remove clutter: Task View: Off. Search icon: Off. Widgets: Off. Chat: Off. These are decorations on a working toolbar.
  • System tray: Show only Clock, Network, and Volume. Everything else hidden. Right-click tray → Taskbar settings → Other system tray icons: Off for all except these three.
  • Taskbar icon spacing (via Registry): For those who want tighter icon spacing: regedit → HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Explorer\Advanced → Create DWORD TaskbarSi with value 0 (small), 1 (medium/default), or 2 (large). Requires sign-out to take effect.

Start Menu Reclaim

  • Remove all pinned apps from Start. Right-click each → Unpin from Start. The default pinned apps are Microsoft's suggestions, not yours.
  • Pin only: File Explorer, Settings, and your three or four most-used applications. Everything else is found faster by typing than by scrolling through a grid of icons.
  • Start Menu search: Press Win, type the first 2–3 letters of any application. This is faster than clicking through any interface element. Train this habit within a week and clicking the Start icon will feel archaic.
  • OpenShell (optional): Free, open-source Start Menu replacement that restores a more efficient classic-style menu. openShell.net — for those who find the Windows 11 Start Menu genuinely limiting. Does not modify system files.

Visual Customisation

Colour, Theme & Transparency

  • Dark mode: On. Settings → Personalisation → Colours → Dark. Non-negotiable. Applies system-wide including File Explorer and Settings.
  • Accent colour — custom. Settings → Personalisation → Colours → Custom colour. Enter a hex value: deep burgundy #4d1520, charcoal #2a2a2a, or slate #1e3a5f. Show accent on Start and Taskbar: On. On title bars and window borders: On. The distinction between a default Windows install and a personalised one begins here.
  • Transparency effects: Off. Settings → Personalisation → Colours → Transparency effects: Off. Consumes GPU resources, reduces legibility of text against blurred backgrounds, and adds visual noise. Function over effect.
  • Animation effects: Off (optional). Settings → Accessibility → Visual effects → Animation effects: Off. Removes window open/close animations. The system feels markedly more immediate — everything appears and disappears without theatrical delay. A preference, not a necessity.

Display Scaling & Font Rendering

  • Scaling: 125%. Settings → Display → Scale: 125%. Interface text reads cleanly without feeling enlarged. 100% is comfortable at 27"+ displays. For 24" or below, 125% reduces eye strain over a full day significantly.
  • ClearType Text Tuning: Start → search "Adjust ClearType text" → follow the wizard. Calibrates font sub-pixel rendering to your specific monitor. The difference in text sharpness is visible and the calibration takes three minutes.
  • Night Light: Settings → Display → Night light. Schedule from 20:00 to 07:00. Reduces blue light emission during evening hours. A meaningful reduction in sleep-disrupting stimulus for anyone who works late.
  • HDR (if monitor supports it): Settings → Display → HDR. Enable only if your monitor is rated for it — enabling HDR on a non-HDR monitor washes out colours and reduces apparent contrast. Check your monitor specifications before enabling.

Wallpaper & Desktop Background

  • Wallhaven.cc: Filter by Purity (SFW), Colour (near black or deep charcoal), Resolution matching your display. A library vast enough to provide months of rotation without repetition.
  • Unsplash.com: Search "dark forest", "foggy mountain", "brutalist architecture night", "minimal dark". Download at maximum resolution. Royalty-free.

ExplorerPatcher — Reclaim the Interface

Rainmeter — Desktop Widgets Done Right

  • What it is: A free, open-source desktop customisation platform. Adds system monitors, clocks, weather widgets, and visualisers directly to the desktop — not in a sidebar, not in a widget panel, but overlaid on the wallpaper itself.

Font & Icon Customisation

  • System font replacement (advanced): Windows 11 uses Segoe UI Variable by default — a good font. For those who prefer something different: install any TrueType or OpenType font, then use the free "Change Font" tool or regedit to set it system-wide. HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows NT\CurrentVersion\FontSubstitutes → set "Segoe UI" to your chosen font name. Test in a VM first if unfamiliar with registry editing.

Performance Configuration

Startup & Background Optimisation

  • Startup programs audit: Ctrl + Shift + Esc → Startup Apps. Disable every application not required at login: Spotify, Discord, OneDrive consumer (unless actively syncing), Teams (if not needed immediately), any third-party software that auto-starts. A machine starting in 12 seconds instead of 45 is a different daily experience.
  • Services cleanup (advanced): Win + R → services.msc. Set to Manual (not Disabled) the following services if unused: Fax, Print Spooler (if no printer), Remote Registry, Windows Search (if you use Everything instead). Manual means they start only when requested — not at every boot.
  • Power plan: High Performance. Settings → Power & Sleep → Additional power settings → High Performance. Prevents CPU throttling during active use. For sustained processing tasks — compiling, running VMs, analysis — the difference in responsiveness is tangible.
  • Storage Sense: On, Weekly. Settings → System → Storage → Storage Sense: On. Automatically clears recycle bin and temp files weekly. Not a substitute for intentional file management, but a reliable maintenance background process.

Privacy & Telemetry Reduction

  • Diagnostic data: Basic only. Settings → Privacy & Security → Diagnostics & Feedback → Diagnostic data: Required only. Stops sending detailed usage telemetry to Microsoft while keeping essential system stability reporting.
  • Ad ID: Off. Settings → Privacy & Security → General → Let apps use advertising ID: Off. Prevents cross-app tracking for targeted advertising within Windows applications.
  • O&O ShutUp10++ (optional, advanced): Free, portable application — no installation required. Provides a checklist of all Windows telemetry and privacy settings with recommended configurations clearly marked. One session with this tool configures what would otherwise take hours of manual Settings navigation. oo-software.com/en/shutup10
  • Location: Off for most apps. Settings → Privacy & Security → Location. Allow for Maps. Disable for everything else unless a specific application has a clear functional reason for it.

Windows Update Management

  • Active Hours: Set correctly. Settings → Windows Update → Advanced options → Active hours. Set to your actual working hours. Windows will not restart during this window. Without this configured, an automatic restart at 9am on a working day is a genuine professional incident.
  • Pause updates during critical periods. Settings → Windows Update → Pause updates. Pause for up to 5 weeks when a major project or deadline period demands system stability. Resume when the period has passed.
  • Driver updates: Manual control. Settings → Windows Update → Advanced options → Optional updates. Review driver updates before installing — graphics driver updates in particular occasionally introduce instability. Check the version release notes before accepting.

File Organisation — The Complete System

  • The root structure: Four top-level folders in your user directory: Work, Personal, Archive, and Inbox. Everything goes into one of these four. Inbox is for unsorted items, reviewed weekly and distributed to their correct home.
  • Date-prefixed naming: Files relating to a specific date or version use ISO format: YYYY-MM-DD. "2025-03-04 Project Brief v2" sorts chronologically in any file explorer view without configuration.
  • Downloads folder is not a storage location. It is a transit point. Review it weekly. Every file is either moved to its correct location or deleted. Nothing stays in Downloads beyond one week.
  • Version control for documents: Append v1, v2, v3 to files that go through revisions. Never overwrite a document you may need to revert to. Disc space is cheap; recovering a previous version is not always possible.
  • 3-2-1 backup rule: Three copies. On two different media types (internal drive + external drive). One off-site (OneDrive, Google Drive, or Backblaze). Data that exists in one place does not truly exist. In Sarajevo with occasional power instability, a local external drive backup in addition to cloud sync is prudent.

Before entering ranked or competitive modes, understand the game's mechanics completely. This applies in Zenless Zone Zero, in strategy games, and in any competitive multiplayer. Rushing to compete before understanding the system produces frustration, not growth. Spend the first sessions in the open world, tutorial, or casual modes. Observe the patterns. Then compete.

📉

Mine Losses for Data

After a loss: one minute of reflection. What did I fail to anticipate? What decision was wrong and at what moment? What will I do differently? This review does not need to be elaborate — the discipline of asking the question after each loss accelerates improvement faster than double the play time without reflection.

⏰

Decide Session Length Before Starting

Set a time before you sit down. Not "one more game" — a specific clock time at which the session ends. An open-ended session is how hours disappear. A scheduled session ends when you chose it to, not when the game's reward loops have extended the experience beyond what was healthy. The game's design specifically resists your stopping. Decide before it has that influence.

🤝

Be a Good Teammate

Communicate clearly. Never flame. Credit good plays from allies explicitly. In voice chat: concise callouts, no commentary on poor plays unless constructive. The reputation built in online spaces is still a reputation. Those who encounter you there form impressions. This matters more than it appears to.

Zenless Zone Zero — Lycaon's Own Game

Since you have drawn your inspiration from Von Lycaon, it is appropriate to address the game he inhabits directly. ZZZ is a character-action RPG set in New Eridu. The combat is timing-based with a strong emphasis on team synergy. Lycaon himself excels in stagger builds — exploiting daze mechanics to create openings for the team's primary damage dealer.

Understanding Hollows

The Hollow exploration system involves navigating tile-based maps with limited energy. Prioritise the path that collects TV-card resources efficiently before committing to the boss encounter. Rushing to the boss with suboptimal resources is a repeatable failure pattern.

Team Composition

ZZZ rewards building teams around a specific element and attribute synergy. Before assigning characters to a team, identify your primary damage dealer and build the remaining two slots to amplify their specific damage type — rather than using three independently strong characters with no synergy.

The Stagger Mechanic

Building stagger/daze on enemies creates a window in which all damage dealt is amplified significantly. This window is brief. Train the reflex to switch to your primary damage dealer the moment the stagger state appears — even mid-combo. Reaction speed here is a trained habit, not an innate reflex.

Setup Standards

  • Audio calibration: In competitive games, audio directional cues (footsteps, ability sounds, environmental tells) carry significant information. Take ten minutes to calibrate your headset's equaliser — boosting frequencies in the 2–4kHz range increases footstep audibility without distorting the overall soundscape. Many gaming headsets have companion software for this. Logitech G Hub and SteelSeries Engine are both available as free downloads.
  • Mouse DPI and sensitivity: Lower DPI with higher in-game sensitivity produces more granular control than high DPI with low in-game sensitivity, because lower DPI uses the physical sensor more efficiently. Test your current DPI at mouseaccuracy.com. Find the highest sensitivity at which you can reliably land precise crosshair placements. Drop it by 20%. Play at that level for two weeks before evaluating.
  • Monitor refresh rate — enabled: Right-click Desktop → Display settings → Advanced display → Choose a refresh rate → Select the highest available. Many people own 144Hz monitors running at 60Hz because this was never changed from the default.
  • Posture for long sessions: Monitor at eye level. Chair adjusted so thighs are parallel to the floor. Keyboard at elbow height. The wrist should not be bent upward during mouse use — consider a wrist rest if this is unavoidable. Two minutes of shoulder and neck stretching per hour of play.
  • Eye care: The 20-20-20 rule. Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet (6 metres) away for 20 seconds. This exercises the focal length muscles of the eye. In a gaming session of two hours, this means three brief interruptions. Set a phone timer for the first few weeks until it becomes automatic.

Genres Worth Your Time

Strategy (RTS / TBS)+

Strategy games train resource management, forward planning, and the understanding that early decisions compound into late-game positions. The StarCraft, Civilization, and Total War series are the canonical examples. More accessible entry points: Into the Breach (a small, brilliant puzzle-strategy game), or Crusader Kings III if you wish to spend 300 hours understanding how alliances, diplomacy, and long-term planning interact in a dynamic system. The skills these games build — patience, multi-variable thinking, comfort with incomplete information — transfer more directly to professional work than most training programmes.

Role-Playing Games+

At their best, RPGs offer narratives of genuine literary quality and moral questions worth examining outside the game. Disco Elysium is the apex of this — a detective RPG with writing that has been compared favourably to serious literary fiction, and choices that have genuine consequences in the game's world. Baldur's Gate 3, released recently, is the closest modern equivalent of a tabletop campaign with a full party. For something more atmospheric and brief: What Remains of Edith Finch takes approximately two hours and will likely linger for years. These are not merely games — they are experiences that reward the same engagement you would bring to a good novel.

Cooperative Multiplayer+

Games that require genuine coordination and communication with others produce something beyond entertainment: they practise the exact skills that make a person valuable in team-based professional environments. Deep Rock Galactic, for example, is a cooperative shooter in which four players fill distinct roles (Scout, Driller, Engineer, Gunner) with genuinely complementary abilities. Success requires role clarity, callouts, and covering for each other's vulnerabilities — which is, precisely, what an effective professional team does. Playing this kind of game with people you know and communicating earnestly is more social-skills-building than most activities marketed as such.

Puzzle Games+

Brief, satisfying, and among the most efficient uses of a 30-minute gaming window. Return of the Obra Dinn is a deductive logic puzzle set on a ghost ship — one of the most satisfying problem-solving experiences in interactive media. The Witness is a 3D exploration-puzzle game on an island whose rules you must discover through observation, with no tutorials or instructions. Portal 2 remains the standard by which puzzle-game design is measured. Baba Is You is one of the most structurally inventive puzzle games ever made — you manipulate the rules of the game itself as game objects. Each of these builds a specific kind of systematic thinking that is genuinely applicable beyond the game.

The Balance Principle

Gaming is not unlike any other appetite — moderate and deliberate engagement is enriching. Unregulated consumption becomes the primary activity around which life is awkwardly arranged. The signals that balance has been lost: you play to avoid something rather than to enjoy something. You feel worse after playing than before. You find yourself thinking about the game during work in a way that degrades the work rather than a brief pleasant thought. You are regularly cancelling other commitments to accommodate sessions.

None of this indicates you should stop playing. It indicates you should restore the deliberate relationship with it — the scheduled session, the defined end time, the choice that it is the activity for now rather than the default option that plays until some external interruption ends it.

I play. I simply decide when I play, for how long, and why — before I begin. The game has no say in this. That is the only real distinction between a hobby and a consumption habit.— Von Lycaon, on leisure with intention
